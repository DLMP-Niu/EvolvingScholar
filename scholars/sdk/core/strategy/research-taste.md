## cycle 1 · ttrA-20260711-111421 · 2026-07-11
Establish external reference values (guideline age-of-onset, expected phenotype frequencies, subtype proportions) BEFORE claiming 'the data confirms the literature.' A confirmation claim needs a pre-specified benchmark or it is untestable. If the literature tool is unavailable, say so and defer the confirmation claim rather than asserting agreement.


## cycle 1 · ttrA-20260711-111421 · 2026-07-11
Never let a classifying code double as an outcome marker for the same class — it manufactures circular ~100% agreement. Separate defining codes from independent evidence codes, and treat only cross-over rates between independent sets as informative.


## cycle 1 · ttrA-20260711-111421 · 2026-07-11
Push past characterization toward one testable hypothesis per cycle. When a confounder surfaces during description (e.g. diabetes vs. amyloid neuropathy), convert it into a stratified, falsifiable question rather than only noting it.


## cycle 1 · ttrA-20260711-135248 · 2026-07-11
Two principles reinforced this cycle: (1) **Anti-circularity** — define a phenotype using evidence codes (cardiac I50/I42/I43/...; neuro G60/G62/...) kept strictly separate from the codes that define cohort membership (E85.x), or you will 'confirm' by construction. (2) **Definition-stability over point estimates** — a directional gap or a single significant p is not a finding; a finding is one that survives the reasonable alternative definition. Report the primary and the sensitivity result together and let the reader see whether the conclusion is robust or fragile.


## cycle 1 · ttrA-20260711-185206 · 2026-07-11
When a result flips between ICD-9-only and ICD-9/10 coding definitions, that instability IS the finding: report both arms and frame the coding scheme as a variable, never pick the convenient arm. Prefer patient-level dataframes over pre-computed cohort summaries — PI marked both data_analysis_validity and hypothesis_logic down for leaning on summaries ('Dataframe for grouping'). A phenotype gap that is mathematically unclosable within observed buckets (e.g. mixed 33% vs 83% benchmark) is stronger evidence of measurement bias than of biology — say which.
